\begin{abstract}
  We present \package, an open-source Python package implementing the
  Wilson--Daubechies--Meyer (WDM) wavelet-packet time-frequency transform for
  gravitational-wave data analysis.  The WDM basis and its applications
  to gravitational-wave astronomy were established in foundational
  work by Klimenko, Necula, Cornish, and collaborators; the goal of
  this paper is to provide a self-contained, mathematically explicit
  reference for practitioners wishing to adopt the WDM in modern
  Python and JAX inference workflows.  We document the forward and
  inverse transform step by step, making explicit the packed
  $(N_t, N_f{+}1)$ coefficient layout, the DC and Nyquist edge-channel
  conventions, the alternating phase factor $C_{nm}$, and the
  normalization conditions required for exact round-trip reconstruction.
  The package supplies NumPy and JAX backends, both validated to
  floating-point precision, with the JAX backend enabling
  GPU-accelerated transforms of million-point data streams in tens of
  milliseconds.  As a worked example we verify that the WDM-domain
  Whittle likelihood reproduces frequency-domain posteriors for a
  resolved LISA galactic binary under a shared stationary noise model,
  confirming numerical equivalence of the two representations in that
  controlled setting.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}





Time-frequency analysis is widely used in gravitational-wave
astronomy. It enables template-free characterisation of source
signals~\cite{krolak_1996,Virtuoso2024}, instrumental
transients~\cite{robinet2020}, and the joint analysis of
astrophysical signals and terrestrial
disturbances~\cite{Cornish_2015}. Time-frequency
representations also provide compact waveform descriptions and
facilitate the modelling of non-stationary detector noise. Among the
available techniques, discrete wavelet-packet transforms are
particularly well suited to gravitational-wave data analysis: they
furnish localized, orthonormal basis functions capable of representing
transient and chirping signals, and for locally stationary noise they
yield an approximately diagonal noise covariance, greatly simplifying
likelihood evaluation and spectral estimation.

The Wilson--Daubechies--Meyer (WDM) wavelet-packet basis already
has a substantial history in gravitational-wave astronomy.  The fast
Wilson--Daubechies transform was developed for transient
gravitational-wave analysis by Necula, Klimenko, and
Mitselmakher~\cite{Necula_2012}, and WDM representations have been
used in coherent WaveBurst and related burst-search contexts.
% TODO(Avi): Add citation for coherent WaveBurst WDM implementation.
These earlier papers and implementations established the theoretical
and algorithmic basis of the transform for excess-power and
time-frequency analyses, including its orthonormal structure,
controlled spectral leakage, flexible frequency sub-bands, and
time-delay filters relevant for sky localization.  Subsequent WDM
software and applications by Digman and Cornish further developed fast
forward and inverse transforms, WDM likelihoods, and time-frequency
analyses for LISA sources and backgrounds~\cite{2023ascl.soft07037D,
digman_cornish_bbh_wdm,digman_cornish_tv_galactic_wdm}.  Recent work
by Pearson and Cornish has also shown how WDM-domain methods can be
combined with Bayesian data augmentation to handle data gaps in
long-duration gravitational-wave observations~\cite{pearson_cornish_wdm_gaps}.

Cornish developed a complementary WDM-domain programme for
likelihood-based gravitational-wave inference and waveform generation.
In this formulation, regularly sampled data are mapped to an
orthogonal grid of localized time-frequency pixels; slowly evolving
signals may be represented compactly as tracks on this grid, and
binary waveforms can be generated directly in the WDM domain
\cite{Cornish:2020odn}.  A central statistical motivation is that
locally stationary noise, whose spectrum varies slowly across an
individual time-frequency pixel, has an approximately diagonal
covariance in the WDM domain.  This approximate diagonalization,
analyzed in detail for the WDM basis in
Ref.~\cite{cornish_nonstationary_wdm}, provides the WDM-domain
analogue of the familiar diagonal Fourier-domain covariance for
stationary noise and underlies WDM Whittle-type likelihoods.

The present manuscript does not claim to introduce the WDM transform,
to replace these foundational analyses, or to outperform existing
pipeline-specific implementations.  Its contribution is instead to
make the sampled WDM construction easy to adopt and reproduce in
modern Python-based gravitational-wave workflows.  We give a
self-contained derivation of the forward and inverse transforms,
state the packed $(N_t,N_f+1)$ coefficient layout explicitly, spell
out the DC and Nyquist channel treatment, and fix the normalization,
phase, and indexing conventions used by the implementation.  These
choices are then validated through exact round-trip reconstruction
tests and empirical checks of coefficient decorrelation.  The
accompanying \package\ package provides CPU and GPU-capable NumPy/JAX
backends, so that the same mathematically explicit transform can be
used in conventional analyses and in differentiable inference
workflows.

The motivation for the WDM can be understood by contrasting the two
standard representations of a discrete signal. In the time domain one
can identify \emph{when} a feature occurs but not which frequency
band is active, whereas in the Fourier domain one can identify
\emph{which} frequencies are present but not when they appear. Many
gravitational-wave signals and detector artefacts are neither purely
stationary nor purely impulsive, so neither representation alone is
fully satisfactory. A short-time Fourier transform partially addresses
this limitation through a sliding window, but imposes a fixed
time-frequency resolution across the entire dataset. The WDM transform
instead tiles the time-frequency plane with localized atoms indexed by
a time bin $n$ and a frequency channel $m$, with coefficients $w_{nm}$
measuring the projection of the data onto each atom. The result is a
uniform time-frequency representation well suited to describing
transients, chirps, gaps, and other localized structure, while still
admitting an exact inverse transform back to the original sampled data.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:conventions} introduces notation and conventions.
Section~\ref{sec:wdm} develops the WDM transform, including the Meyer
window, the full three-case basis definition covering DC, interior,
and Nyquist channels, the forward and inverse transforms, and the
statistical properties of the coefficients under stationary and
non-stationary noise. Section~\ref{sec:code_package} describes the
\package\ package, presents runtime and accuracy benchmarks, and
demonstrates the transform on astrophysical signals. We conclude in
Section~\ref{sec:conclusions}.

\subsection{Conventions}\label{sec:conventions}
Consider a data stream consisting of $N$ uniformly sampled values of a
\textbf{real} quantity $x$ at times $\{t_k\}_{k=0,\dots,N-1}$ over a
total observation time $T$:
\begin{align}\label{st_definition}
  x[k]=x(t_k)\qquad,\qquad k=0,\dots,N-1\;,
\end{align}
with sampling interval $\Delta t = t_{k+1} - t_{k}$ and $T = N\Delta t$.
All available information is encoded in these $N$ samples.
We define the sampling rate and the Nyquist frequency as
\begin{align}
  r_s&\equiv\frac{1}{\Delta t}\equiv\frac{N}{T}\;,\nonumber\\
  f_{\rm max}&=\frac 1 2r_s\;,\nonumber\\
  \Delta f &= 1/T
\end{align}
In that regard, if we set $t_0=0$, we can write $t_k=k\Delta t$.
In the frequency domain, the data stream $\{x_k\}$ can be
discrete-Fourier-transformed to a set of other $N$ complex numbers as
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:dft-definition}
  \tilde x[l] = \sum_{k = 0}^{N-1} x[k]e^{-2\pi i l k/N}
\end{equation}
for Fourier frequencies $f_{l} = \{-N\Delta f/2, \ldots, -\Delta f,
0, \Delta f, \ldots, (N/2 - 1)\Delta f\}$, i.e.\ the zero-frequency
bin is centred in the array.


\subsection{Time and Frequency Domains}
We assume that the output of a gravitational wave detector, $x(t)$,
is expressed as a linear combination of a signal, $h(t| \vec{\mu})$,
determined by a finite set of (unknown) parameters, $\vec{\mu}$, and
instrumental noise, $n(t)$. If we ignore the presence of calibration
errors~\cite{Savalle_2022} \textcolor{blue}{which paper should it be?}, the content of a single data stream,
i.e., one output channel from one detector, can be written in the
time and frequency domain as:
\begin{align}
  {x}[k] & = h[k|\vec{\mu}] + n[k],\\
  \tilde x[l]& = \tilde{h}[l|\vec{\mu}]+ \tilde{n}[l]
\end{align}
where the tilde indicates the Fourier transform.

In a gravitational wave context it is usual to further assume that
the instrumental noise follows a Gaussian distribution characterized
by a one-sided PSD, $S_n(f)$, defined such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:psd_definition}
  \langle \tilde{n}^*[l] \tilde{n}[l'] \rangle = \frac{1}{2} P\big[|l|\big] \delta_{ll'},
\end{equation}
where the expectation value $\langle \, \rangle$ is taken over the
data generating process. The delta function in the previous equation
implies that different frequencies are not correlated.

In reality the noise model is not known perfectly and could vary from
the assumption above in a number of ways. For example, the PSD might
have a different shape from the reference one \cite{babak2021lisa} \textcolor{blue}{which paper should it be?},
the probability distribution of the noise might not be Gaussian, or
the noise might not be stationary, leading to correlations between frequencies.

\section{The Wilson--Daubechies--Meyer (WDM) Transform}\label{sec:wdm}

\subsection{Terminology}
The name Wilson--Daubechies--Meyer combines several pieces of
time-frequency analysis terminology. A standard discrete wavelet
transform usually refers to a multiresolution, dyadic decomposition
(``dyadic'' meaning organized by factors of two, so that each
successive scale changes the time and frequency resolution by powers
of two). Such transforms represent low-frequency structure with broad
time support and fine frequency resolution, and high-frequency
structure with narrower time support and coarser frequency
resolution. A \emph{wavelet packet} (or \emph{wavelet-wavepacket})
transform is more general: it allows a broader class of admissible
tilings of the time-frequency plane. The object studied in this paper
is the Wilson--Daubechies--Meyer (WDM) wavelet-packet transform; for
brevity, we refer to it as the WDM transform throughout. Unlike a
standard dyadic wavelet transform, the WDM transform used here has a
uniform rectangular tiling, so that all pixels have the same time
width $\Delta T$ and frequency width $\Delta F$, with
$\Delta T\,\Delta F = 1/2$ under the one-sided frequency convention
used below.

The word ``Wilson'' refers to the Wilson-basis construction, in which
positive- and negative-frequency windowed Fourier atoms are combined
with alternating phase factors to form a real, orthonormal basis. This
is the origin of the paired terms centered at $+m\Delta F$ and
$-m\Delta F$, and of the phase factor $C_{nm}$ in the basis definition
below. The ``Daubechies--Meyer'' part refers to the smooth Meyer-type
frequency window used to construct these atoms. In the present
implementation this is the cosine-tapered $d=1$ Meyer window defined
below.

The WDM transform maps the original $N$ samples to a packed
coefficient grid with $N_t$ time bins and $N_f+1$ stored frequency
channels. The transform size is still set by
\begin{align}\label{NtNf_def}
  N=N_t\,N_f\;,
\end{align}
and the stored channels are indexed by $m=0,\dots,N_f$, with $m=0$
the DC edge channel, $m=1,\dots,N_f-1$ the interior channels, and
$m=N_f$ the Nyquist edge channel. The underlying idea is to partition the data stream into $N_t$ time
segments and to perform a frequency analysis of each one. Here $N$ is
fixed by the data, while $N_t$ is a free user choice subject only to
$1 \le N_t \le N$ (and consequently $1 \le N_f \le N$).
Given that choice we can define the spacing in both time and
frequency with respect to the underlying tiling with area $N = N_{t}N_{f}$
\begin{align}\label{DTDF}
  \Delta T&\equiv\frac{T_{\text{obs}}}{N_t}\; \qquad \text{grid time
  spacing},\nonumber\\
  \Delta F&\equiv\frac{f_{\rm max}}{N_f} \qquad \text{grid frequency spacing}\,.
\end{align}
One immediately sees that,
\begin{align}
  \Delta T &= \frac{T_{\text{obs}}}{N_{t}} = \frac{N\Delta t}{N_{t}}
  = N_{f}\Delta t \\
  \Delta F &= \frac{f_{\text{max}}}{N_{f}} = \frac{1}{2N_{f}\Delta t}=\frac{N_t\Delta f}{2} = \frac{1}{2\Delta T}
\end{align}
This final expression shows how the Nyquist theorem still holds. Once
you fix $N_t$ (or equivalently $N_f$, $\Delta T$ or $\Delta F$) the
grid is uniquely identified. Figure~\ref{fig:wdm_packed_grid}
summarizes the packed layout, including the DC and Nyquist edge
channels and the alternating phase factor used for the interior
coefficients.

\subsection{Forward transform}
For a given frequency series $\tilde x[l]$, the WDM transform represents the data
through the coefficients
\begin{align}
  \label{wnm_def}
  w_{nm}&=\sum_{l=0}^{N-1}\tilde x[l]\tilde g^*_{nm}[l]\;,\nonumber\\
  \tilde x[l]&=\sum_{n=0}^{N_t}\sum_{m=0}^{N_f}w_{nm}\tilde g_{nm}[l]\;,
\end{align}
where $n$ corresponds to the time index and $m$ corresponds to the
frequency-channel index. The functions $g_{nm}[k]$ are the WDM
basis elements, which are chosen to be (quasi-)orthonormal:
\begin{align}\label{eq:orthonormality}
\sum_{l=-N/2}^{N/2-1}g^*_{nm}[l]g_{pq}[l]=\delta_{(nm),(pq)}\;,\nonumber\\
\sum_{n=0}^{N_t-1}\sum_{m=0}^{N_f}g^*_{nm}[l]g_{nm}[l']=\delta_{ll'}\;,
\end{align}
This orthonormality is the key reason that WDM coefficients can
become statistically simple for stationary noise. If the
Fourier-domain noise satisfies Eq.~\eqref{eq:psd_definition}, then
the covariance between two WDM noise coefficients is
\begin{align}
\left\langle w_{nm} w_{pq} \right\rangle = \frac{1}{2}\sum_l P[l]\,\tilde g_{nm}[l]\tilde g_{pq}^*[l].
\end{align}

In the ideal orthonormal limit, whitened noise — or more generally
noise with $P[l]$ effectively constant across the support of each
atom — reduces this covariance to diagonal form. In that case the WDM
coefficients are uncorrelated, and for Gaussian noise they are
therefore independent. In the sampled packed implementation used here,
it is useful to distinguish two statements. First, the interior atoms
($m=1,\ldots,N_f-1$) satisfy the discrete orthonormality relation to
machine precision. Second, the packed real-valued storage includes
special DC and Nyquist edge channels, so the full stored coefficient
array is best viewed as a convenient packed representation rather than
a minimal orthonormal basis.
For this reason, the decorrelation discussion below focuses on the
interior coefficients. For whitened noise their empirical
correlations remain very small, while for colored, non-stationary, or
non-Gaussian noise off-diagonal structure becomes more visible and
the coefficients should be regarded as only approximately decorrelated
rather than strictly independent.
Figure~\ref{fig:wdm_orthogonality_decorrelation} illustrates these points
numerically for a small sampled transform and for white and colored
noise realizations.

The basis elements are defined in closed form in the frequency
domain. For interior channels ($0 < m < N_f$), DC ($m=0$), and
Nyquist ($m=N_f$) the definitions differ:
\begin{align}
  \label{eq:gnm_full}
  \tilde{g}_{nm}(f) =
  \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{cases}
    e^{-4\pi i n f \Delta T}\, \tilde{\varphi}(f), & m=0, \\[6pt]
    e^{-2\pi i n f \Delta
    T}\Big(C_{nm}\,\tilde{\varphi}(f - m\Delta F) & \\[2pt]
    \quad + C_{nm}^*\,\tilde{\varphi}(f + m\Delta F)\Big), & 0 < m <
    N_f, \\[6pt]
    e^{-4\pi i n f \Delta T}\Big(\tilde{\varphi}(f - N_f\Delta F) & \\[2pt]
    \quad + \tilde{\varphi}(f + N_f\Delta F)\Big), & m = N_f,
  \end{cases}
\end{align}
%
for $n = 0,\ldots,N_t-1$. The interior channels carry a $1/\sqrt{2}$
normalization and the alternating phase factor $C_{nm}$, while the DC
and Nyquist edge channels have a doubled time-shift exponent and no
$C_{nm}$ modulation. These edge channels each contribute $N_t$ real
degrees of freedom to the packed grid, accounting for the
$N_t(N_f+1)$ stored entries versus the $N = N_tN_f$ independent samples.

The window $\tilde\varphi$ is the Meyer window, defined in the
frequency domain by
\begin{align}\label{eq:meyer_window}
  \tilde\varphi(f) &=
  \begin{cases}
    \frac{1}{\sqrt{\Delta \tilde{F}}} & |f|<A \\
    \frac{1}{\sqrt{\Delta \tilde{F}}} \cos \left[\frac{\pi}{2}\,\left(
    \frac{|f|-A}{B}\right)\right] & A \leq|f| \leq A+B \\
    0 & \text{otherwise,}
  \end{cases}
\end{align}
where the frequencies are discretized as
\begin{equation}
  f_l = \frac{l}{N \Delta t}
\end{equation}
By construction, $\tilde\varphi$ has a limited frequency bandwidth
which is controlled by the parameter $A$. In this work, we restrict
attention to the $d=1$ case, for which the Meyer window reduces to
the cosine taper written above; this is also the case implemented in
the current package, while $d$ is retained in the API only for
possible future
generalizations.\footnote{Cornish~\cite{Cornish:2020odn} uses $d=4$,
  which yields a flatter passband and steeper roll-off at the expense
  of a wider time-domain support (the window function must be truncated
  at $\pm q\Delta T$ with $q=16$). The $d=1$ cosine taper used here has
  a gentler roll-off but is more compact in time, requiring a smaller
  $q$. In practice, the choice of $d$ trades spectral leakage
  suppression against temporal compactness of the window; for the
  applications considered in this paper the $d=1$ window provides
  adequate frequency separation while keeping the implementation simple
and the time-domain filter short.} The parameter $\Delta \tilde{F}$
depends on the frequency resolution
$\Delta \tilde{F} = \Delta F \Delta t$.
The parameter $B$ depends on $\Delta\Omega$ and $A$ as
\begin{equation}
  B = \Delta F - 2A
\end{equation}
In our implementation, we set $A =\Delta \tilde{F}/4$ and therefore $B =
\Delta \tilde{F}/2$. This means that the window is non-zero when
$|f| \leq 3 \Delta \tilde{F}/4$. 

These are the main ingredients behind the packed WDM grid. A single
window shape is shifted across
frequency to define the channel index $m$, and each shifted window is
then modulated in time to define the atom indexed by $n$. This is why
one WDM coefficient can be read as the strength of one localized
time-frequency pattern, rather than as the amplitude of a globally
supported sinusoid.

More concretely, a single coefficient $w_{nm}$ answers the question:
``how much does the data resemble the atom centered at time bin $n$
and frequency channel $m$?'' A large magnitude means that the data
contain a feature with roughly the same time localization, central
frequency, and oscillatory phase structure as that atom. Reading the
WDM plane coefficient-by-coefficient in this way is often much more
intuitive than interpreting a global Fourier series, because the
index pair $(n,m)$ already carries an approximate time-frequency location.

We now derive the practical computational form of the forward WDM
coefficients by inserting the basis functions~\eqref{eq:gnm_full}
into the definition~\eqref{wnm_def}. The phase factor $C_{nm}$
alternates between real and imaginary depending on $n+m$:
\begin{align}
  C_{nm}=e^{\frac{i\pi}{4}(1-(-1)^{n+m})} =
  \begin{cases} 1 & n+m \text{ even,} \\ i & n+m \text{ odd.}
  \end{cases}
\end{align}
We treat the three channel types separately.

\subsubsection{Interior channels ($0 < m < N_f$)}

Starting from Eq.~\eqref{wnm_def} with the interior-channel basis
element from Eq.~\eqref{eq:gnm_full}, the forward transform can be
evaluated in the Fourier domain using
\begin{align}
  w_{nm} = \sum_{l=-N/2}^{N/2-1} \tilde{x}_l\,\tilde{g}_{nm}^*(f[l])\,,
\end{align}
where $\tilde{x}_l$ is the DFT of the input data. For a
real-valued input signal, $\tilde{x}_{-l} = \tilde{x}_l^*$, and the
window satisfies $\tilde{\varphi}(-f) = \tilde{\varphi}(f)$.
Splitting the sum into positive and negative frequency halves and
using these symmetries, the negative-frequency contribution is the
complex conjugate of the positive-frequency contribution (see
Appendix~\ref{app:forward_derivation} for details), giving
\begin{align}\label{eq:wnm_interior}
  w_{nm} &=
  \sqrt{2}\,(-1)^{nm}\,\Re\left[C_{nm}^*\sum_{l'=-N_t/2}^{N_t/2}\tilde{x}_{l'+mN_t/2}\,e^{2\pi
  i l'n/N_t}\,\tilde{\varphi}(l'\,df)\right],
\end{align}
where $l' = l - mN_t/2$ and $df = 1/T$. The frequency shift
$l = l' + mN_t/2$ contributes a phase $e^{2\pi i (mN_t/2) n/N_t}
= e^{i\pi mn} = (-1)^{mn}$, so the result can be written compactly as
\begin{align}\label{eq:wnm_compact}
  w_{nm} = \sqrt{2}\,(-1)^{nm}\,\Re\,C_{nm}^*\,x_m[n]\,,
\end{align}
where
\begin{align}\label{eq:xm_def}
  x_m[n] = \sum_{l=-N_t/2}^{N_t/2-1} e^{2\pi i l n/N_t}\,X[l +
  mN_t/2]\,\tilde{\varphi}[l]
\end{align}
is the windowed, shifted inverse DFT of length $N_t$. The quantity
$x_m[n]$ can be computed using a length-$N_t$ FFT for each channel
$m$, so the total cost of the interior channels is $N_f \times
\mathcal{O}(N_t\log N_t)$.

\subsubsection{DC channel ($m = 0$)}

The DC edge channel has a doubled time-shift exponent and no $C_{nm}$
factor in the basis definition~\eqref{eq:gnm_full}. Following the
same Fourier-domain evaluation yields
\begin{align}\label{eq:wn0}
  w_{n0} = \sqrt{2\,}\Re\left[\sum_{l=1}^{N_t/2}\tilde{x}_l\,e^{4\pi i l
  n/N_t}\,\tilde{\varphi}(l\,df)\right] + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\tilde{x}_0\,\tilde{\varphi}(0)\,.
\end{align}
Note the factor of $4\pi$ in the exponent (versus $2\pi$ for interior
channels): the DC basis element oscillates at twice the rate in the
time index because it sees both positive and negative frequency
copies of the window centered at $f=0$.

\subsubsection{Nyquist channel ($m = N_f$)}

The Nyquist edge channel is handled analogously to the DC channel but
centered at the Nyquist frequency $f_{\rm max}$. Its structure
mirrors the DC case with appropriate frequency shifts. In practice,
the Nyquist channel is computed using the same windowed-FFT machinery
as the interior channels, with the frequency shift set to $mN_t/2 = N/2$.
\begin{align}\label{eq:wnNf}
  w_{nN_f} &= \sqrt{2}\,\Re\!\left[\sum_{l=N/2-N_t/2}^{N/2-1}
    \tilde{x}_l\,e^{4\pi i l n/N_t}\,\tilde{\varphi}\!\left((l-N/2)\,df\right)\right]
    \nonumber\\
  &\quad + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\,\tilde{x}_{N/2}\,\tilde{\varphi}(0)\,.
\end{align}
\subsection{Backward transform}

The original data stream is recovered by applying the backward
(inverse) WDM transform to the coefficients:
\begin{align}
  \label{eq:inverse_wavelet_decomposition}
  x[k]=\sum_{n=0}^{N_t-1}\sum_{m=0}^{N_f}w_{nm}\,g_{nm}[k]\,.
\end{align}
Equivalently, in the Fourier domain the reconstruction reads
\begin{align}\label{eq:inverse_fourier}
  \tilde{x}_l = \sum_{n=0}^{N_t-1}\sum_{m=0}^{N_f}\tilde{g}_{nm}[l]\,w_{nm}\,.
\end{align}
That this is indeed the inverse of the forward transform follows from
the (quasi-)orthonormality of the basis: inserting the forward
transform~\eqref{wnm_def} into~\eqref{eq:inverse_fourier} and
using~\eqref{eq:orthonormality} returns $\tilde{x}_l$ up to the
overlap of adjacent window functions. Specifically, the sum over $n$
produces $N_t\delta_{ll'}$ factors via the discrete orthogonality of
the complex exponentials, and the sum over $m$ covers the full
frequency axis because adjacent windows satisfy
$\tilde{\varphi}^2(\omega) + \tilde{\varphi}^2(\omega - \Delta\Omega)
= 1/\Delta\Omega$ in their overlap region (proven in
Appendix~\ref{app:normalization_Meyer}). Together these two
identities ensure exact round-trip reconstruction: forward followed
by inverse returns the original data to machine precision, as
verified numerically in Section~\ref{sec:code_package}.

\subsection{Statistical properties and the WDM likelihood}\label{sec:stats}

The practical payoff of the (approximate) orthonormality and
decorrelation discussed above is that standard gravitational-wave
data analysis quantities --- the likelihood, signal-to-noise ratio,
and Fisher information matrix --- can all be written as simple sums
over WDM pixels, in direct analogy with the familiar frequency-domain
expressions.

\paragraph{From covariance to likelihood.}
For Gaussian noise characterized by a covariance matrix $\mathbf{C}$,
the log-likelihood of the data $\mathbf{d}$ given a signal model $\mathbf{h}$ is
\begin{align}
  \ln p(\mathbf{d}|\mathbf{h}) =
  -\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{d}-\mathbf{h})^\top
  \mathbf{C}^{-1}(\mathbf{d}-\mathbf{h}) - \frac{1}{2}\ln\det(2\pi\mathbf{C})\,.
\end{align}
In the Fourier domain with stationary noise, $\mathbf{C}$ is diagonal
with entries proportional to the power spectral density $S(f_l)$, so
the likelihood reduces to a sum over frequency bins --- the Whittle
likelihood. The same simplification occurs in the WDM domain whenever
the noise covariance is (approximately) diagonal, which, as argued
above, holds for locally stationary noise whose PSD varies slowly
compared to the frequency support of each atom. In that regime the
WDM noise covariance takes the form
\begin{align}\label{eq:wdm_noise_cov}
  \langle w_{nm}^{\mathrm{noise}}\, w_{pq}^{\mathrm{noise}} \rangle
  \approx S_{nm}\,\delta_{np}\,\delta_{mq}\,,
\end{align}
where $S_{nm}$ is the \emph{evolutionary power spectral density}
evaluated at the time-frequency location of pixel $(n,m)$. For
stationary noise, $S_{nm}$ reduces to the usual PSD integrated over
the atom's frequency support~\cite{Cornish:2020odn}:
\begin{align}\label{eq:snm_stationary}
  S_{nm} \approx S(f_m)\,\Delta F\,.
\end{align}
Substituting the diagonal covariance~\eqref{eq:wdm_noise_cov} into
the general Gaussian likelihood yields the WDM Whittle log-likelihood:
\begin{align}\label{eq:wdm_whittle}
  \ln p(d|h) = -\frac{1}{2}\sum_{n,m}\left[\ln(2\pi S_{nm}) +
  \frac{(d_{nm} - h_{nm})^2}{S_{nm}}\right],
\end{align}
where $d_{nm}$, $h_{nm}$ are the WDM coefficients of the data and
signal model, respectively. This expression is structurally identical
to the standard frequency-domain Whittle likelihood, but with the
one-dimensional sum over frequency bins replaced by a two-dimensional
sum over time-frequency pixels. The key advantage is that $S_{nm}$
can vary with time, naturally accommodating non-stationary noise
without requiring off-diagonal terms in the covariance.

\paragraph{Inner product, SNR, and Fisher matrix.}
The noise-weighted inner product in the WDM domain follows directly
from~\eqref{eq:wdm_whittle}:
\begin{align}\label{eq:wdm_inner}
  (a \,|\, b) = \sum_{n,m} \frac{a_{nm}\,b_{nm}}{S_{nm}}\,,
\end{align}
from which the matched-filter signal-to-noise ratio is
\begin{align}
  \rho(h) = \frac{(d \,|\, h)}{\sqrt{(h \,|\, h)}}\,,
\end{align}
and the Fisher information matrix is
\begin{align}\label{eq:wdm_fisher}
  \Gamma_{ij} = \sum_{n,m} \frac{\partial_{i} h_{nm}\;\partial_{j}
  h_{nm}}{S_{nm}}\,.
\end{align}
These expressions assume that the diagonal
approximation~\eqref{eq:wdm_noise_cov} holds. When it does not ---
for example, when the noise PSD varies rapidly compared to $\Delta
T$, or when the noise is strongly non-Gaussian --- the off-diagonal
covariance terms must be retained and the simple pixel-wise sums
above become inadequate. Figure~\ref{fig:wdm_orthogonality_decorrelation}
provides a practical
diagnostic: if the off-diagonal entries of the empirical covariance
remain small after whitening, the diagonal
likelihood~\eqref{eq:wdm_whittle} is a safe approximation.

\paragraph{When does the diagonal approximation hold?}
The WDM tiling obeys $\Delta T\,\Delta F = 1/2$ (see
Eq.~\eqref{DTDF}), so each atom occupies the minimum allowed
time-frequency area consistent with the uncertainty principle, though
the actual time-bandwidth product of the cosine-tapered Meyer window
exceeds this minimum. The diagonal covariance~\eqref{eq:wdm_noise_cov}
holds whenever the noise PSD varies slowly compared to this joint
support. Concretely, this is the condition of \emph{local
stationarity}: spectral variations on timescales shorter than $\Delta
T$, or spectral features narrower than $\Delta F$, will induce
off-diagonal covariance terms. The WDM tiling therefore poses an
inherent trade-off: choosing finer frequency resolution (larger
$\Delta T$) suppresses spectral leakage within each atom but degrades
temporal tracking of non-stationarity. In practice a single choice of
$N_f$ often suffices for mildly non-stationary noise, but data with
sharp spectral features or rapid temporal variation may require a
multi-resolution strategy, as discussed by
Cornish~\cite{Cornish:2020odn}.

\section{\package\ Package}
\label{sec:code_package}

The \package\ package provides NumPy and JAX backends for the
Wilson--Daubechies--Meyer (WDM) transform, both implementing the
packed $(N_t, N_f+1)$ coefficient layout and forward/inverse
conventions described above.  The primary interface exposes the full
forward and inverse transforms; in addition, band-limited routines
allow the transform to be evaluated over a specified subset of
frequency channels, reducing compute cost when only a portion of the
time-frequency plane is needed.

From the implementation point of view, the dominant operations in the
forward transform are one length-$N$ FFT of the input data together
with one length-$N_t$ sub-band transform for each stored channel. This
gives a cost of
\begin{align}
  T_{\rm forward} = \mathcal{O}(N \log N) + \mathcal{O}(N \log N_t),
\end{align}
which is asymptotically $\mathcal{O}(N \log N)$ because $N=N_tN_f$
and $N_t \leq N$. The inverse transform has the same scaling up to
constant factors: it reconstructs the full spectrum from the packed
channels and then applies one inverse FFT back to the time domain. In
both directions, the stored coefficient grid contains $\mathcal{O}(N)$
real numbers, and the temporary working arrays used by the
implementation are also linear in $N$, so the overall memory footprint
is $\mathcal{O}(N)$.

We validate the implementation using round-trip reconstruction tests.
For an input time series $x$, we compute its packed WDM coefficients,
apply the inverse transform, and compare the reconstructed series
$\hat{x}$ with the original. Across the benchmarked problem sizes and
backends, the relative reconstruction error
\begin{align}
  \epsilon_{\rm rt} = \frac{\|\hat{x}-x\|_2}{\|x\|_2}
\end{align}
remains at the level of double-precision floating-point roundoff,
$\epsilon_{\rm rt} \lesssim 10^{-16}$, providing a direct numerical
check that the packed layout, including the DC and Nyquist edge
channels, is implemented consistently across both backends.

All timings are drawn from the benchmark suite
included in the package documentation. Each data point reports the
median wall-clock time over 7 repeated runs in 64-bit floating-point
arithmetic; JAX JIT compilation is excluded via a single warmup
evaluation, and GPU timings assume arrays already resident on the
device (host--device transfer is not included). Benchmarks were
performed on Google Colab using a CPU node (2$\times$ Intel Xeon
vCPU, 2.0--2.2\,GHz, 12.7\,GB RAM) and a GPU node (NVIDIA T4,
16\,GB VRAM).

Figure~\ref{fig:runtime_forward} shows the forward-kernel runtime as a
function of input size $N$ for the NumPy (CPU), JAX (CPU), and JAX
(GPU) backends, together with the batched-execution speedup
$t_\mathrm{serial}/t_\mathrm{batch}$. Here, $t_\mathrm{serial}$ is
the wall-clock time for three independent single-channel calls and
$t_\mathrm{batch}$ is the time for a single call that processes all
three channels simultaneously.

At large $N$ all three backends approach $\mathcal{O}(N\log N)$
scaling, consistent with the dominant length-$N$ FFT in the forward
transform.  Deviations at small $N$ reflect different overhead
regimes: per-call Python and memory-allocation costs for NumPy, and
kernel-dispatch latency for JAX.  The JAX GPU runtime is nearly flat
below $N \approx 10^5$, indicating that transforms at those sizes are
dominated by kernel-launch overhead rather than arithmetic throughput;
beyond this threshold GPU efficiency improves and, at $N = 10^6$, the
GPU scalar runtime (${\approx}\,13\,\mathrm{ms}$) is roughly
$35\times$ lower than NumPy (${\approx}\,460\,\mathrm{ms}$).

The speedup panel shows that batched execution yields no measurable
benefit for NumPy ($t_\mathrm{serial}/t_\mathrm{batch} \approx 1$
throughout), as NumPy does not exploit the extra batch dimension for
additional parallelism.  JAX (CPU) recovers $1.5$--$3\times$ via
JIT-compiled vectorisation over the batch axis.  JAX (GPU) achieves
up to ${\sim}\,4\times$ at small $N$, where all three channels fit
simultaneously into the device pipeline; the speedup decreases to
${\approx}\,1.5\times$ at $N = 10^6$ as individual transforms
approach device saturation.  The $\epsilon_{\rm rt} \lesssim 10^{-16}$
round-trip errors established above hold for all backends and sizes;
the runtime differences therefore reflect implementation efficiency
rather than any loss of numerical fidelity.

These benchmarks characterise the present NumPy and JAX implementations
under the conventions used in this paper, rather than providing an
exhaustive comparison with existing pipeline-specific WDM codes.  The
main conclusion is that the JAX backend can substantially reduce the
cost of large WDM transforms while preserving the same floating-point
reconstruction accuracy as the NumPy implementation.

\subsection{Resolved galactic-binary inference in a galactic background}
\label{sec:lisa_gb_example}


As an end-to-end inference example, we generated a one-year LISA-like
dataset containing stationary instrumental noise, a stochastic galactic
foreground, and one resolved galactic binary (GB). Both the
frequency-domain and WDM-domain likelihoods use the same stationary
noise model throughout; this is an intentional controlled comparison
designed to isolate the effect of the WDM basis change from any
modelling differences. The effective PSD $S_c(f)$ in each channel $c$
includes both the instrumental noise contribution and the
time-averaged galactic foreground. Figure~\ref{fig:lisa_gb_data} shows
the characteristic-strain comparison between the injected source and
the channel noise model, and
Figure~\ref{fig:lisa_gb_corner} shows the overlaid posterior
constraints from the two analyses.

The data set is constructed on a frequency grid spanning
$10^{-4}$--$3\times 10^{-3}\,\mathrm{Hz}$. The unresolved galactic
foreground is described by the phenomenological PSD \cite{Karnesis:2021tsh}
%
\begin{align}
  S_{\rm gal}(f) &=
  A_{\rm gal} f^{-7/3}
  \exp\!\left[-\left(\frac{f}{f_{1,{\rm gal}}}\right)^{\alpha_{\rm gal}}\right]
  \nonumber\\
  &\quad\times
  \left[1 + \tanh\!\left(\frac{f_{{\rm knee},{\rm gal}} - f}{f_{2,{\rm gal}}}\right)\right],
\end{align}
%
using a standard smooth fit for the LISA galactic foreground. To
project this foreground into the detector channels we combine that
frequency model with an anisotropic Milky Way sky map obtained by
line-of-sight integration through the bulge-plus-disk density model \cite{Mentasti:2023uyi}
%
\begin{align}
  \rho_{\rm gal}(x,y,z) =
  \rho_0\left[
    A e^{-r^2/R_b^2}
    + (1-A)e^{-u/R_d}\,{\rm sech}^2(z/Z_d)
  \right],
\end{align}
%
where $r^2=x^2+y^2+z^2$ and $u^2=x^2+y^2$. We evaluate the
time-dependent detector response using equal-arm LISA orbits~\cite{lisaorbits}
sampled at $90$ orbital times, and use that response to map the
anisotropic foreground into the A,E,T TDI channels, assuming that the
spectrum is stationary within each of these $90$ orbital segments. We
then draw complex Gaussian realisations of both the instrumental noise
and the chunked galactic foreground and stitch them into a full-year
spectrum. The resolved source is injected afterward as a TDI~1.5 A,E,T
waveform computed with \texttt{jaxgb}~\cite{jaxgb}, using equal-arm
LISA orbits and local Fourier support $n=256$. In the realization shown here the cadence is $\Delta t = 166.7\,\mathrm{s}$, $T_{\rm obs}=1\,\mathrm{yr}$, and the injected binary has $f_0 = 1.3862897\,\mathrm{mHz}$, $\dot f = 1.32\times 10^{-18}\,\mathrm{Hz\,s^{-1}}$, $A = 1.19\times 10^{-23}$, and $\phi_0 = 1.442\,\mathrm{rad}$.

The resolved GB model uses the standard eight source parameters
$(f_0,\dot f,A,\alpha,\delta,\psi,\iota,\phi_0)$, but for this
controlled comparison we sample only
$(f_0,\dot f,A,\phi_0)$ and hold sky position and orientation fixed to
their injected values. The carrier frequency is drawn near the fixed
reference value $f_{0,\rm ref}=1.38591\,\mathrm{mHz}$ through
$\delta\!\ln f_0 \sim \mathcal{U}[-10^{-3},10^{-3}]$, while
$\dot f$ and $A$ are drawn from truncated Gaussians in log space over
$[5\times 10^{-19},\,4\times 10^{-18}]\,\mathrm{Hz\,s^{-1}}$ and
$[6\times 10^{-24},\,1.7\times 10^{-23}]$, respectively.

The frequency-domain analysis uses a local A/E/T band centered on the
prior support of the source and evaluates the Whittle likelihood
\begin{align}
  \ln \mathcal{L}_{\rm freq}(\theta)
  &\propto
  -\sum_{c\in\{A,E,T\}} \sum_k
  \left[
    \ln S_c(f_k)
    + \frac{2\Delta f\,\bigl|\Delta t\,[d_{c,k}-h_{c,k}(\theta)]\bigr|^2}{S_c(f_k)}
  \right],
\end{align}
where $d_{c,k}$ and $h_{c,k}$ are the complex local Fourier modes and
$S_c(f)$ is the stationary effective PSD, namely instrumental noise\footnote{\textcolor{red}{We adopted the Gaussian two-parameters model for the instrumental noise PSD, following equations (B14) and (B15) in \cite{LISACosmologyWorkingGroup:2022kbp}}}
plus the time-averaged galactic
foreground contribution in channel $c$ \textcolor{red}{Shall we expand a bit on how we generate the galactic background here?}. The WDM analysis starts from
the same local Fourier patch, embeds the template into that
patch, and applies the WDM transform with
$N_t=32$, $a=1/3$, and $d=1$. The resulting WDM likelihood is the
diagonal Gaussian specialization of Eq.~\eqref{eq:wdm_whittle},
\begin{align}
  \ln \mathcal{L}_{\rm WDM}(\theta)
  &= -\frac{1}{2}\sum_{c\in\{A,E,T\}} \sum_{n,m}
  \Bigg[
    \ln(2\pi \sigma^2_{c,m})
    \nonumber\\
  &\qquad\qquad
    + \frac{\left(w^{(d)}_{c,nm}-w^{(h)}_{c,nm}(\theta)\right)^2}{\sigma^2_{c,m}}
  \Bigg],
\end{align}
with a stationary per-pixel variance following from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:snm_stationary} with $\Delta F = f_{\rm Nyq}/N_f$:
\begin{align}
  \sigma^2_{c,m} = S_{nm} = S_c(f_m)\,\Delta F = \frac{S_c(f_m)}{2\Delta t} = f_{\rm Nyq}\,S_c(f_m),
\end{align}
broadcast across the WDM time bins. In other words, this example uses
the same stationary PSD in both domains and asks whether the WDM
transform reproduces the frequency-domain posterior under that shared
noise model.

Both analyses use the NumPyro~\cite{phan2019composable} implementation
of the No-U-Turn Sampler with a dense mass matrix, target acceptance
probability $0.85$, and maximum tree depth $10$. Each run uses $800$ warmup steps followed by
$1000$ retained samples from a single chain, initialized near the
injected parameters with small jitters in $\log \dot f$ and $\log A$.
In the realization shown here, both samplers produced zero divergences. The
latent effective sample sizes are $613$--$637$ for the
frequency-domain chain and $590$--$714$ for the WDM chain, while the
corresponding $\hat R$ values lie in the ranges
$0.999$--$1.007$ and $0.999$--$1.016$. The truth-band optimal SNRs are
$22.5469$ in the frequency-domain calculation and $22.5462$ in the
WDM calculation. All reported $90\%$ credible intervals contain the
injected truth in both runs, and the posterior medians differ only by
$9.0\times 10^{-11}\,\mathrm{Hz}$ in $f_0$,
$2.2\times 10^{-20}\,\mathrm{Hz\,s^{-1}}$ in $\dot f$,
$5.2\times 10^{-26}$ in $A$,
$6.1\times 10^{-3}\,\mathrm{rad}$ in $\phi_0$, and
$0.10$ in SNR. Within the numerical precision of this study, the WDM
and frequency-domain analyses are therefore statistically
indistinguishable for this seeded resolved-GB example.

foreground was generated; sky position and orientation were then held
fixed at their injected values during sampling. Both likelihoods use
the same time-averaged effective PSD throughout, so this study
isolates the effect of the WDM basis change from any difference in
noise modelling: the non-stationarity of the galactic foreground
enters both analyses as the same shared systematic and does not
contribute to the JSD. For each seed we computed the Jensen--Shannon

These results confirm that the WDM-domain likelihood faithfully
reproduces the frequency-domain posterior under a shared stationary
noise model, across a representative population of signal parameters
and noise realisations.

The \package\ package enables both a straightforward NumPy-based
development path and high-performance accelerator execution via JAX.
For small data lengths ($N \lesssim 2^{20}$), the NumPy version is
competitive or even faster due to its extremely low overhead.
However, once $N$ exceeds $\sim 2^{20}$, the GPU-accelerated versions
achieve markedly lower runtimes while both backends maintain
round-trip reconstruction errors at floating-point roundoff.
This combination of flexibility, accuracy, and performance makes
\package\ a useful tool for WDM time-frequency analysis of
long-duration gravitational-wave signals.

\section{Conclusions}\label{sec:conclusions}

We have presented a self-contained, pedagogical account of the
Wilson--Daubechies--Meyer (WDM) wavelet-packet transform, building on
the theoretical foundations established in prior
work~\cite{Necula_2012,Klimenko:2005xv,Klimenko:2015ypf,Cornish:2020odn} \textcolor{blue}{citations broken},
and its implementation in the open-source \package\ package.  The
primary goal of this work is to make the discrete, sampled WDM
implementation explicit and reproducible for practitioners new to the
transform, by walking through the full three-case basis definition
(DC, interior, and Nyquist channels), the packed $(N_t, N_f{+}1)$
coefficient layout, the forward and inverse transforms with detailed
derivations, and the conditions under which WDM coefficients may be
treated as approximately independent.

On the implementation side, the NumPy and JAX backends both achieve
exact round-trip reconstruction to floating-point precision. The JAX
backend provides substantially lower runtimes for large transforms,
making it practical to incorporate WDM-domain operations into
inference pipelines that require millions of likelihood evaluations.

Several limitations of the current work should be noted. First, the
package implements only the $d=1$ Meyer window; higher-order windows
($d > 1$) offer better spectral leakage suppression and may be
preferable for data with sharp spectral features. Second, the paper
does not address the generation of gravitational-wave signals directly
in the WDM domain — where much of the computational advantage of the
WDM representation lies~\cite{Cornish:2020odn} — and we defer fast
WDM-domain waveform generation to future work. Third, the
treatment of data gaps and edge effects, while conceptually
straightforward, requires careful handling of the boundary basis elements
and is not yet implemented in the package. Fourth, the LISA galactic-binary
inference example of Section~\ref{sec:lisa_gb_example} uses a
time-averaged effective PSD in both the frequency-domain and
WDM-domain likelihoods; this controlled comparison isolates the WDM
basis change but does not exercise the principal advantage of the WDM
representation, namely the ability to assign a time-varying $S_{nm}$
to each pixel and thereby accommodate genuinely non-stationary noise
without off-diagonal covariance terms. Demonstrating this advantage on
realistic LISA non-stationary backgrounds is an important direction for
future work.

Looking ahead, the most immediate extensions are the incorporation of
fast WDM-domain waveform generation for compact binary signals,
the implementation of evolutionary power spectral density estimation
directly on the WDM grid, and integration of the package into
existing LISA data analysis frameworks.  The combination of a
pedagogical reference, a tested open-source implementation, and
GPU-accelerated performance should lower the barrier to adopting the
WDM transform within modern, autodiff-compatible Python inference
pipelines for the long-duration, non-stationary signals expected from
future detectors such as LISA.

\section{Normality condition of the $g_{nm}$ basis}
We introduce the basis $\tilde g_{nm}(f)$ in the discrete case:
%
\begin{align}
\label{eq:gnm_discr}
\tilde{g}_{nm}[l] =
\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{cases}
e^{-4\pi i n l/N_t}\, \tilde{\varphi}[l], & m=0, \\[6pt]
e^{-2\pi i n l/N_t}\Big(C_{nm}\,\tilde{\varphi}[l - mN_t/2] + C_{nm}^*\,\tilde{\varphi}[l + mN_t/2]\Big), & 0 < m < N_f, \\[6pt]
e^{-4\pi i n l/N_t}\Big(\tilde{\varphi}[l - N/2] + \tilde{\varphi}[l + N/2]\Big), & m = N_f,
\end{cases}
\end{align}
%
where we write the Meyer window function in the discrete case focusing on $d=1$:
%
\begin{align}\label{eq:phi_discrete}
\tilde\varphi[l]&=\sqrt{\frac{2}{N_t}}\begin{cases}
1 & |l_r|<A \\
\cos \left[\frac{\pi}{2}\,\left(
\frac{|l_r|-A}{B}\right)\right] & A \leq|l_r| < A+B \\
0 & |l_r|\geq A+B
\end{cases}\nonumber\\
l_r&=l\frac{2}{N_t}
\end{align}
%
where $l\in\{-\frac{N_t}{2},-\frac{N_t}{2}+1,...,\frac{N_t}{2}-1\}$. Now we verify that
%
\begin{align}
\sum_{nm}&\tilde{g}_{nm}[l]\tilde{g}_{nm}^*[l']=\frac{1}{2}\sum_n e^{-4\pi i n (l-l')/N_t}\, \tilde{\varphi}[l]\,\tilde{\varphi}[l']\nonumber\\
&+\frac{1}{2}\sum_{0<m<N_f}\sum_{n}e^{-2\pi i n (l-l')/N_t}\,\Big(C_{nm}\,\tilde{\varphi}[l - mN_t/2] + C_{nm}^*\,\tilde{\varphi}[l + mN_t/2]\Big)\Big(C_{nm}^*\,\tilde{\varphi}[l' - mN_t/2] + C_{nm}\,\tilde{\varphi}[l' + mN_t/2]\Big)\nonumber\\
&+\frac{1}{2}\sum_n e^{-4\pi i n (l-l')/N_t}\Big(\tilde{\varphi}[l - N/2] + \tilde{\varphi}[l + N/2]\Big)\Big(\tilde{\varphi}[l' - N/2] + \tilde{\varphi}[l' + N/2]\Big)
\end{align}
%
Now we simplify the $0<m<N_f$ term, by noticing that $C_{nm}^2=C_{nm}^{*2}=(-1)^{m}(-1)^{n}$ and $|C_{nm}|^2=1$
%
\begin{align}
\frac{1}{2}\sum_{0<m<N_f}\sum_{n}&e^{-2\pi i n (l-l')/N_t}\,\Big(\tilde{\varphi}[l - mN_t/2]\tilde{\varphi}[l' - mN_t/2] + \tilde{\varphi}[l + mN_t/2]\tilde{\varphi}[l' + mN_t/2]\nonumber\\
&+ (-1)^{m}(-1)^{n}\tilde{\varphi}[l - mN_t/2]\tilde{\varphi}[l' + mN_t/2]+ (-1)^{m}(-1)^{n}\tilde{\varphi}[l + mN_t/2]\tilde{\varphi}[l' - mN_t/2]\Big)
\end{align}
%
one notices that the summation over $n$ can be directly performed, and it vanishes for every value but $l=l'$. In particular, the second line (the mixed terms) always vanishes. A similar result holds for the $m=0,N_f$ cases, therefore we can write
%
\begin{align}
\sum_{nm}&\tilde{g}_{nm}[l]\tilde{g}_{nm}^*[l']=\delta_{ll'}\frac{N_t}{2}\left(\tilde\varphi^2[l]+\sum_{0<m<N_f}\tilde\varphi^2[l-mN_t/2]+\sum_{0<m<N_f}\tilde\varphi^2[l+mN_t/2]+\tilde\varphi^2[l-N/2]+\tilde\varphi^2[l+N/2]\right)
\end{align}
%
Now the thing to notice is that each value of $l$ lays in two and only two of these windows. Let be $\bar m$ and $\bar m+1$ the indices of these two windows, such that $\frac{\bar m N_t}{2}<|l|<\frac{(\bar m+1)N_t}{2}$ ($\bar m$ could be 0, or $\bar m+1$ can be $N_f$). Then the sum becomes
%
\begin{align}
\sum_{nm}&\tilde{g}_{nm}[l]\tilde{g}_{nm}^*[l']=\delta_{ll'}\frac{N_t}{2}\left(\tilde\varphi^2[l-\bar m N_t/2]+\tilde\varphi^2[l-(\bar m+1)N_t/2]\right)
\end{align}
%
Let $l\geq 0$ for sake of simplicity ($l<0$ cases are analogous). Then either $l<\frac{(m+A)N_t}{2}$ or $l\geq\frac{(m+A)N_t}{2}$.
In the first case, we have $\tilde\varphi^2[l-\bar m N_t/2]=\frac{2}{N_t}$ and $\tilde\varphi^2[l-(\bar m+1)N_t/2]=0$, since for $|\frac{2l}{N_t}-(\bar m+1)|>A+B$. In the second case, we have
%
\begin{align}
\sum_{nm}&\tilde{g}_{nm}[l]\tilde{g}_{nm}^*[l']=\delta_{ll'}\left(\cos^2\left[\frac{\pi}{2}\frac{\frac{2l}{N_t}-\bar m -A}{B}\right]+\cos^2\left[\frac{\pi}{2}\frac{\bar m + 1 -\frac{2l}{N_t} -A}{B}\right]\right)
\end{align}
%
which after some algebra simplifies to
%
\begin{align}
\sum_{nm}&\tilde{g}_{nm}[l]\tilde{g}_{nm}^*[l']=\delta_{ll'}\left(1+\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\frac{1-2A}{B}\right)\cos\left(2\frac{\pi}{2}\frac{\frac{2l}{N_t}-\bar m -A}{B}-\frac{\pi}{2}\frac{1-2A}{B}\right)\right)=\delta_{ll'}
\end{align}
%
where we used the fact of $2A+B=1$. Therefore we find that for every value of $l$ we have%
%
\begin{align}
\sum_{nm}&\tilde{g}_{nm}[l]\tilde{g}_{nm}^*[l']=\delta_{ll'}
\end{align}
%

\subsection{Normalization of the Meyer window}\label{app:normalization_Meyer}

We verify that the Meyer window defined in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:phi_discrete} (the discrete case of \eqref{eq:meyer_window}) is correctly normalized. We compute
%
\begin{align}
\sum_{l=-N/2}^{N/2-1} \tilde{\varphi}^2[l]&=2\sum_{l=0}^{A\frac{N_t}{2}-1}\frac{2}{N_t}-\frac{2}{N_t}+2\sum_{l=A\frac{N_t}{2}}^{(A+B)\frac{N_t}{2}-1}\frac{2}{N_t}\cos^2\left[\frac{\pi}{2}\frac{\frac{2l}{N_t}-A}{B}\right]=2A-\frac{2}{N_t}+\frac{4}{N_t}\sum_{k=0}^{B\frac{N_t}{2}-1}\cos^2\left[\frac{\pi}{2}\frac{2k}{BN_t}\right]=\nonumber\\
&=2A-\frac{2}{N_t}+\frac{4}{N_t}\sum_{k=0}^{B\frac{N_t}{2}-1}\frac{1}{2}+\frac{4}{N_t}\sum_{k=0}^{B\frac{N_t}{2}-1}\frac{1}{2}\cos\left(\frac{2\pi k}{BN_t}\right)=2A-\frac{2}{N_t}+B+\frac{2}{N_t}=1
\end{align}
%
where on the first line the term $-\frac{2}{N_t}$ avoids double counting $l=0$, where the last summation equals $+\frac{2}{N_t}$ after performing the geometrical series and where we remember that $2A+B=1$ is a property of the window function.

\section{Detailed derivation for the forward and inverse transforms}\label{app:forward_derivation}

This appendix provides the full algebraic steps leading to the
compact expressions for the forward WDM coefficients presented in
Section~\ref{sec:wdm}. We work in the Fourier domain throughout,
writing the forward transform as
\begin{align}
  w_{nm}=\sum_{l=-N/2}^{N/2-1} \tilde{x}_l\,\tilde{g}_{nm}^*(f[l])\,,
\end{align}
where the discrete frequencies are mapped as $f[l]=l\,df=l/T$.

\subsection{Interior channels ($0<m<N_f$)}

Inserting the interior-channel basis element from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:gnm_full} and splitting the sum over positive and
negative frequencies gives
\begin{align}
w_{nm}&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sum_{l=0}^{N/2-1} \tilde{x}_l\, e^{2\pi iln/N_t}\left[C_{nm}^*\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l-\tfrac{mN_t}{2})df\right)+C_{nm}\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l+\tfrac{mN_t}{2})df\right)\right]\nonumber\\
&+\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sum_{l=-N/2}^{-1} \tilde{x}_l\, e^{2\pi iln/N_t}\left[C_{nm}^*\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l\!-\!\tfrac{mN_t}{2})df\right)+C_{nm}\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l\!+\!\tfrac{mN_t}{2})df\right)\right].
\end{align}
Using the reality
condition $\tilde{x}_{-l} = \tilde{x}_l^*$ together with the
symmetry $\tilde{\varphi}(-f) = \tilde{\varphi}(f)$, the second sum
is the complex conjugate of the first. The boundary terms at $l=0$
and $l=-N/2$ vanish because the window support does not extend to
those frequencies for interior channels. We therefore obtain
\begin{align}\label{eq:app_wnm_general}
  w_{nm}&=\sqrt{2}\,\Re\sum_{l=1}^{N/2-1} \tilde{x}_l\, e^{2\pi
  iln/N_t}\,C_{nm}^*\,\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l-\tfrac{mN_t}{2})df\right)\nonumber\\
  &=\sqrt{2}\,(-1)^{nm}\,\Re\sum_{l'=-N_t/2}^{N_t/2}
  \tilde{x}_{l'+mN_t/2}\, e^{2\pi
  il'n/N_t}\,C_{nm}^*\,\tilde{\varphi}(l'\,df)\,,
\end{align}
where the last equality follows from the substitution $l' = l -
mN_t/2$ and the fact that $\tilde{\varphi}(l'\,df)$ is nonzero only
for $|l'| \leq N_t/2$. This is Eq.~\eqref{eq:wnm_interior} of the
main text, and corresponds (up to normalization conventions) to
Eq.~17 of Cornish~\cite{Cornish:2020odn}.

\subsection{DC channel ($m = 0$)}

For the DC edge channel, the basis element has a doubled time-shift
exponent and no $C_{nm}$ factor. The same positive/negative frequency
splitting yields
\begin{align}
  w_{n0}&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sum_{l=0}^{N/2-1} \tilde{x}_l\, e^{4\pi
  iln/N_t}\,\tilde{\varphi}(l\,df)
  +\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sum_{l=-N/2}^{-1} \tilde{x}_l\, e^{4\pi
  iln/N_t}\,\tilde{\varphi}(l\,df)
  \nonumber\\
  &=\,\sqrt{2}\Re\left[\sum_{l=1}^{N_t/2} \tilde{x}_{l}\, e^{4\pi
  iln/N_t}\,\tilde{\varphi}(l\,df)\right]+\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\tilde{x}_0\,\tilde{\varphi}(0)\,,
\end{align}
which is Eq.~\eqref{eq:wn0} of the main text.
\subsection{Nyquist channel ($m=N_f$)}

\begin{align}
w_{nm}&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sum_{l=0}^{N/2-1} \tilde{x}_l\, e^{2\pi iln/N_t}\left[\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l-\tfrac{N}{2})df\right)+\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l+\tfrac{N}{2})df\right)\right]+
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sum_{l=-N/2}^{-1} \tilde{x}_l\, e^{2\pi iln/N_t}\left[\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l\!-\!\tfrac{N}{2})df\right)+\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l\!+\!\tfrac{N}{2})df\right)\right]\nonumber\\
&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sum_{l=0}^{N/2-1} \tilde{x}_l\, e^{2\pi iln/N_t}\left[\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l-\tfrac{N}{2})df\right)+\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l+\tfrac{N}{2})df\right)\right]+
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sum_{l=1}^{N/2} \tilde{x}_{-l}\, e^{-2\pi iln/N_t}\left[\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(-l\!-\!\tfrac{N}{2})df\right)+\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(-l\!+\!\tfrac{N}{2})df\right)\right].
\end{align}
%
the $l=0$ term in the first summation vanishes, and the $l=N/2$ term is treated separately:
%
\begin{align}
  w_{nm}&=\sqrt{2}\,\Re\sum_{l=1}^{N/2-1} \tilde{x}_l\, e^{2\pi
  iln/N_t}\,\,\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l-\tfrac{N}{2})df\right)+\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\tilde{x}_{N/2}\, e^{-\pi
  inN_f}\tilde{\varphi}(0)\nonumber\\
  &=\sqrt{2}\,\,\Re\sum_{l'=-N_t/2}^{-1}
  \tilde{x}_{l'+N/2}\, e^{2\pi
  il'n/N_t}\,\tilde{\varphi}(l'\,df)+\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\tilde{x}_{N/2}\, \tilde{\varphi}(0)\,,
\end{align}
%
assuming an even value for $N_f$.

\subsection{The inverse transform}

We write the inverse transform of the $w_{nm}$ coefficients to recover the signal $x[l]$ for $l\geq 0$ since we know that for $l<0$ the reality condition holds ($x[-l]=x^*[l]$):
%
\begin{align}
x[l]=\sum_{n=0}^{N_t-1}\sum_{m=0}^{N_f}w_{nm}g_{nm}[l]&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sum_{n}\sum_{0<m<N_f}w_{nm}e^{-2\pi iln/N_t}\left[C_{nm}\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l-\tfrac{mN_t}{2})df\right)+C^*_{nm}\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l+\tfrac{mN_t}{2})df\right)\right]\nonumber\\
&+\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sum_{n}w_{n0}e^{-4\pi iln/N_t}\tilde{\varphi}(l\,df)+\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sum_{n}w_{nN_f}e^{-4\pi iln/N_t}\left[\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l-\tfrac{N}{2})df\right)+\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l+\tfrac{N}{2})df\right)\right]=\nonumber\\
&=\begin{cases}
x_0[l]\qquad\qquad\qquad \qquad l<N_t/2\\
x_m[l]\qquad\qquad\qquad m N_t/2\leq l<(m+1)N_t/2\\
x_{N_f}[l]\qquad\qquad\qquad l \geq N/2-N_t/2
\end{cases}
\end{align}
%
where we note that for each value of $l$, the inverse transform depends on which of the $m$ bands the $l$ value belongs, in particular
%
\begin{align}
x_0[l]&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sum_{n}w_{n1}e^{-2\pi iln/N_t}C_{n1}\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l-\tfrac{N_t}{2})df\right)+\sum_{n}w_{n0}e^{-4\pi iln/N_t}\tilde{\varphi}(l\,df)\nonumber\\
x_m[l]&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sum_n w_{nm}e^{-2\pi iln/N_t}C_{nm}\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l-\tfrac{mN_t}{2})df\right)+\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sum_n w_{n(m+1)}e^{-2\pi iln/N_t}C_{n(m+1)}\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l-\tfrac{(m+1)N_t}{2})df\right)\nonumber\\
x_m[l]&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sum_n w_{n(N_f-1)}e^{-2\pi iln/N_t}C_{n(N_f-1)}\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l-\tfrac{N}{2}+\tfrac{N_t}{2})df\right)+\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sum_n w_{n N_f}e^{-4\pi iln/N_t}\tilde{\varphi}\!\left(\!(l-\tfrac{N}{2})df\right)
\end{align}
